Defining Romanticism

"romantic" is a vague word, whereas "Romantic" is a precise category of literary history

The word "Romantic" comes from "romance" -> A fictional story relating improbable adventures of idealized characters in some remote or enchanted setting. The example is Chrétien de Troyes's King Arthur Tales. (Baldick, Dict. Lit. Terms)

Romantic writers were called "Romantic" because they wanted to read, publish and write like these stories.

The emphasis in Romanticism is in the freedom of individual self-expression : sincerity, spontaneity and originality became standards (Baldick). It is opposed to the standards of Neo-Classicism (cf. Racine etc...)
Romanticism is a set of ideas as well as writers and a period.

The poets are divised in two generations :

  • William Blake; Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge
  • George Gordon ; Lord Byron ; Pery Bysshe Shelley ; John Keats (died 1821).
    Among the most popular were also women (C. Taylor-Smith and T. Hemans)

Romanticism sees literature in a new light, departing from imitation of nature and the classics[1]
Instead it seeks to represent what is within the author.

Our ideas about literature are still marked by Romanticism : we still see poetry as spontaneous and coming from within. Here are a few more ways :

  1. We think literary works have to be "original"
  2. We think poetry is an expression of emotion
  3. We think a literary work creates it's own form

Literature of the XXth and XXIth century either are a continuation of Romanticism or against it[2].

Two key concepts

Originality

The Romantic author is an original genius, the literary work is an expression of individuality.

It is contrasted with the rules and craft of Neo-Classicism. Romantic authors no longer follow rules and imitate the ancients.

Organic form

The Romantic work is seen as an organic form, which grows instead of being assembled. The whole determines the parts -> the whole work changes the words that compose it.

Poets & Readers

In the late 18th century, people are reading less poetry (novels and magazines being popular). Poets want to reaffirm the importance of poetry.
Romanticism then presents characters that are isolated or rebelling against literature. That is reflective of how isolated the poems are from readership. Shelley and Byron exile to the continent.
The poems assert the visionary capacity of poets, and the capacity of poets to reinvent society.

"Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world." Shelley in "A Defence of Poetry" (1821).

This is all at a time when people imagine new ways of leaving together, in the aftermath of the French Revolution.
Much similar to Friedrich's painting, the poet is a wanderer, isolated and in front of a reflexion of his own interiority, vast and mysterious[3].
The sublime view of Nature is a reflection of the creative power of the mind in the power of Nature. It evokes God's power of creation, and the power of a mighty mind.

Imagination

A critic once said, according to Coleridge, that The Rhyme of the Ancient Mariner had "no moral". Coleridge says he finds it has too much; "a work of such pure imagination" shouldn't have a moral at all.

Imagination allows the Mariner to see beauty in ugliness, the invisible in the visible, the whole poem is about it.

In Biographia Literaria (1817), Coleridge makes imagination the criterion of true literature.

Lyrical Ballads (1798) is seen as the beginning of British Romanticism. It explores novelty through the prism of imagination. Two subjects are present : everyday life, seen as novel by the imaginative mind; and the supernatural.

We can see three conceptions of imagination

  1. It is seen as part of perception : it's the active part of perception. This is the idea that when we see something we also create it. It is related to Emmanuel Kant's philosophy, that claims the mind creates time etc...
  2. It is also seen as a "visionary" mode of knowledge. A different way of knowing, unrelated to senses and reason, but only comes from the inside.
  3. It is lastly seen as what defines literary creation : the internal coherence of a work that makes it "organic" and new (see).

(1) is a critique of Empiricism, sees the mind as a lamp rather than a slate or a mirror.
We find this idea in "Lines Written a Few Miles above Tintern Abbey" in Lyrical Ballads (1798). He comes back to a beautiful place where he had been 5 years earlier, depressed. We see the idea that "eye and ear" both perceive and create the world they experience.

(2) is exampled by W. Blake, who seems to have experienced hallucinations, but also by Coleridge's "Kubla Khan". Coleridge knew the unconscious had a role in writing, while being addicted to opium (which he used to treat headaches). Kubla Khan was "composed in a sort of Reverie brought on by Opium". As he was trying to write it down, he was interrupted by a person, and couldn't remember the end. The poem was published as a "fragment".

The poem talks about the Empire of Kubla Khan, a fictitious mongol emperor. It is not composed of a well-ordered sequence of lines and rhyme schemes.
It is a myse en abyme - it's about a poet failing to write what was within him. The fragment represents the unattainable.
It also doesn't tell us what to think of it, doesn't give a moral. It is a record of spontaneous creation.

The world of Kubla Khan is divided between reason and order, masculine : the sun and the garden and savage and passion, feminine : the landscape.
It combines the sexual and the demonic with the sacred and holy. It questions the authority of the rational.

It gives us a picture of a visionary poet, the poem comes from inside as would a sung music. The fountain in the chasm represents inspiration and imagination. The poet is an outsider, frightening because of his visionary power. His political function is dreaming the ideal city, the dialectic of reason and passion.

(3) In Biographia Literaria Coleridge redefines literary imagination. He opposed the memory of "fancy" and the fusion of "imagination" which makes words new and part of an organic whole. Fancy recombines, instead of "growing" a work.
