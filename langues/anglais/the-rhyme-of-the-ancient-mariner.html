The text
About the author :
Samuel Taylor Coleridge

#auteur #litterature #romantisme #anglais

Very well educated (Christ's Hospital in London and Cambridge), student in the 1790s in a revolutionary period. He left university without finishing his studies, planning to establish a socialist community in Pennsylvannia. He was a radical, supported the French Revolution.

He met W. Woodsworth in 1797 near Bristol. They become great friends, compose poems together, and compose Lyrical Ballade, published in 1798 anonymously. Contains the Rhymes of the Ancient Mariner and is the beginning of English Romanticism.

He began using Opium regularly, used his dreams to inspire poems.

In 98-99 he goes to Göttingen to study, is interested in the works of Kant. He comes back to live with Woodsworth then falls into depression & addiction. He fails to find a place in English society (fails as a minister & a journalist, ends up being a sort of lecturer and essayist). In 1816 he is taken in by Dr. James Gilray who takes care of his illness.

In 1817 he publishes Biographia Literaria, an important biography & collection of literary critique. Republishes the A.M., revised and enhanced, adding marginal glosses (prose notes next to the verse)

He dies in 1834. He was a marginal, an outsider, a great literary critic (lectures on Shakespeare), interested in obscure or difficult literature : accounts of voyages, ancient philosophy, German philosophy. He was plagued by nightmares and a feeling of guilt.

Context for The Ancient Mariner

  • Story about a voyage (cf. argument, first page), based on the Magellan's[1]. Important context is the slave trade (which he was against), the colonial order these voyages made possible.

  • Another context clue is modern science, and in particular Newton's view of Nature. Spirits still run around the poem's world, rejecting Newton's and Descartes's view of Nature as mechanical and understandable. The poem then talks about the invisible forces existing in the world.

  • Romanticism is an interest in what cannot be explained by physics (cf. Kant's Critique de la raison pure), in imagination, emotions and creation.

  • Woodsworth expresses a theory of poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings", rejecting the idea that poetry is separated from the poet and merely imitates nature.

3 points : Romanticism, inner life and voyage.

What is a ballad?

Reference to middle ages poems, narrative usually, accompanied by music and usually tragic. They would be preserved by oral tradition and rediscovered, published and received as literature in the 18th, in the context of emerging nationalism (ex. Bishop T. Percy, Reliques of Ancient English Literature, 1763). The literary past is no longer the Classics but the oral tradition.

Educated readers thought these ballads were beautiful and wanted to imitate them -> Lyrical Ballads.

Coleridge's favorite ballad was Sir Patrick Spens. It is marked by a simple form (4 beat lines) and repetition, which evokes a sense of fate, of destiny.

The ballads tell us about tragic events : murder or death. The outcome is known from the beginning, and a kind of fatality is involved. The story is reduced to the essentials, and repetition delays the inevitable. This could be connected to ideas of rituals, of magic.

The stanzas are generally 4 lines, in AB rhyming. The Bs are usually 3 beats, the As usually 4.

Part One of The Ancient Mariner

Archaic language for an archaic mind : the ancient mariner. He is introduced as intemporal "it is an Ancient Mariner", he can't be located in the past, unlike the wedding guests. He is introduced by "it" but is also described as a "man".
Death appears in part 3 : mythical figures & allegories navigate the world.

In part one, we learn that a mariner stops a guest on the way to a wedding to tell the tale of a ship stranded in the ice of the South Pole. An albatros appears out of nowhere and is killed by the teller. The story proper starts around l. 21.
The "Line" is the equator; we'll notice that the Sun is personified, introducing us to a world of myth.

The wedding guest stands for the reader, the ordinary social world. The Mariner is completely other, he comes from a world of myth, sublime and nature.

The last stanza is an interruption by the wedding guest[2], shocked by the Mariner's looks. He seems hanted by guilt, and explains himself for that : he killed the Albatros, and we don't know why. It comes up as a sort of climax.

The Albatros is characterized

In part one, Coleridge is taking us out of the ordinary world, and into a mythical world, where human beings lose control. There is a parallel between the storm and the story, carrying away the boat and the guest. The result is an inexplicable action.
It is also a world of transgression, of crossing the Line, maybe going to far, and "dropping" or falling.
